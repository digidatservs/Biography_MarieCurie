Marie Curie: The first person who got two times a Nobel

A woman who dedicated her life to Science

1867-1890

Maria Sklodowska was born in Warsaw on November 7, the daughter of teachers (her mother died when she was 11 years old) and her father, a math and physics teacher.

She faced the tsarist regime (which excluded women from formal study) at a time when Poland was dominated by Russia. She was part of a group of young people who taught "underground" to working women, and later decided to leave Warsaw to go to Krakow, under Austrian rule.

At 18, she held a position as a governess and financially helped her sister Bronislawa to graduate in Medicine in Paris.

1891-1894

Maria, now in Paris, under the name Marie, undertook to study at the Sorbonne. She met many famous academic physicists, and in 1893 managed to be the first place in the Bachelor of Physics. She worked in laboratories, such as Gabriel Lippman's (physicist, Nobel Prize in 1908) and in 1894, she managed to be second place in the degree in Mathematics.

In the spring in this same year, Marie was introduced to the also renowned physicist Pierre Currie, through an academic meeting promoted by the physicist Józef Wierusz-Kowalski, who noticed an interest in magnetism in both of them.

1895-1897

She married Pierre Curie, who helped her set up her research laboratory, on the university premises, after starting her doctoral thesis after learning about the discoveries of chemist Henri Becquerel, in 1896: an invisible radiation emitted by uranium salts, which was called Becquerel rays.

In 1897, Irène, the Currie's first child, was born. Marie, decided to find out if the phosphorescence of uranium could also be found in other types of materials, and focused her searches on minerals. Marie, deciciu descobrir se a fosforescência do urânio também podia ser encontrada em outros tipos de materiais, e concentrou suas buscas em minerais.

1898-1903

In 1898, Marie and Pierre published articles about their research on two new superior elements in radiation emission to uranium: radium and polonium.

In 1900 she was appointed professor of physics at the École Normale Supérieure for girls in Sèvres.

In 1903, Marie obtained the title of Doctor of Science, shared the Nobel Prize in Physics, in the same year, with Pierre and Becquerel, and was awarded the Davy Medal, from the Royal Society.

1904-1911

In 1904 Éve, the couple's second daughter, is born, and Marie is appointed chief assistant in the laboratory run by Pierre Curie.

In 1906, with the sudden death of Pierre (run over by a cart), Marie had to assume alone the continuity of the researches and was the first woman to become a professor of General Physics at the Faculty of Sciences, after the vacancy of the position, left by your husband.

In 1908 she assumed the position of full professor and in 1910 her fundamental treatise on radioactivity was published.

In 1911, she won the Nobel Prize in Chemistry, for the isolation of pure radium.She also becomes a member of the Conseil du Physique Solvay.

1912-1922

Marie saw the completion of construction of the laboratories of the Institute of Radio (Institut du Radium) at the University of Paris. She promoted, along with her daughter Irène, the use of radio to alleviate the suffering of soldiers during World War I.

Both were dedicated to developing the use of X-Rayography. In 1918, the Radio Institute, which Irène joined the team, began to operate in earnest and would become a universal center for nuclear physics and chemistry.

In 1921, accompanied by her two daughters, Marie Curie was presented with 1 gram of radium by the President of the United States, Warren G. Harding, purchased through a collection among American women.

In 1922, Marie Curie, famously, becomes a member of the Academy of Medicine, with research on the study of the chemistry of radioactive substances and their medical applications.

1913-1927

In 1927, again at the 5th Solvay Conference, in Paris, Marie participated in the famous photo, considered the "smartest of all", for bringing together the most famous scientists of a generation.

Of the 32 personalities present, at least 17 of them won the Nobel.

In the photo, standing in the background—left to right—scientists are Auguste Piccard, Émile Henriot, Paul Ehrenfest, Édouard Herzen, Theophile de Donder, Erwin Schrödinger, Jules-Émile Verschaffelt, Wolfgang Pauli, Werner Heisenberg, Ralph Fowler and Léon Brillouin. In the middle row we have Peter Debye, Martin Knudsen, William Lawrence Bragg, Hendrik Anthony Kramers, Paul Dirac, Arthur Compton, Louis de Broglie, Max Born, and Niels Bohr.

In front, seated from left to right, are Irving Langmuir, Max Planck, Marie Curie, Hendrik Lorentz, Albert Einstein, Paul Langevin, Charles-Eugène Guye, Charles Thomson Rees Wilson, and Owen Richardson.

1929-1930

In 1929, on her second visit to the United States, this time overshadowed by the great stock market crash, Marie received $50,000 in cash (raised from academic colleagues) from President Hoover of the United States to invest in her radioactivity research in your hometown.

Marie understood the importance of having a stockpile of radioactive sources, for applications in medicine as well as for nuclear research.

The stock of 1.5 grams of radium at the Paris Institute of Radium ensured the continuity of research on radium D and polonium in the 1930s, and the experiences that stood out were the studies by Irène Curie and Frédéric Joliot, who carried out early work for the discovery of the neutron, years later, by James Chadwick.

1931-1934

A landmark year in Marie's history, for two reasons: the opportunity to follow her daughter Irène and her husband Frédéric Joliot's discovery of artificial radioactivity and her death, shortly thereafter, of leukemia or, as some sources also report, anemia plastic. Both diseases can be associated with prolonged exposure to chemicals and radioactive elements.

The Curies' legacy is wide, such as the Curie Institute, founded in 1909, in Paris, which houses researchers, doctors and caregivers, who work in 3 locations (Paris, Saint-Cloud and Orsay).

The Curie Museum in Paris and the Museum in Warsaw, Poland.

The University Paris 6 in France is called the Pierre and Marie Curie University and the University in Lublin in Poland is the Marie Curie-Sklodowska University.

Curie's story continued with great prominence for Iréne, and she received several honors aimed at Marie's achievements, including in 1995, the consecration of the ashes of Marie and Pierre Curie at the Pantheon in Paris, being the first woman to be honored for her account. on its own merits.

1935-1956

Irène Curie, following the family's academic and scientific tradition, met and married her laboratory colleague, chemical engineer Frédéric Joliot. This partnership led them to win the Nobel, in 1935, artificially creating radioactive atoms, which culminated in the use of radioisotypes, which became precursors in the treatment of cancer, through nuclear medicine.

Irène, also died of leukemia, on march 17, 1956, at the age of 58, also from extensive exposure to radiation, which occurred mainly after an accidental explosion in her laboratory. Her children also continued in academic and scientific life.
